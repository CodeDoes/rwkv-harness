# Chapter 3: The Ascension

The Council returned with a warrant and twelve operatives. They brought suppression equipment — a modified coil designed to dampen electromagnetic resonance — and a directive that, if Celeste refused to cooperate, the crystal would be taken by force. She read the lattice the night before they were due. It showed her, with dismaying clarity, what suppression would do: the lattice would fragment, the local effects would collapse, and the star-entity would fall into a dormancy that might last the remaining span of her own lifetime.

She could not allow that. Not because she had become invested in the entity — though she had — but because the lattice was not merely "there." It was doing something constructive at a scale she was only beginning to measure: the altered cloud patterns were stabilising regional rainfall, the changed plants were drawing carbon from the atmosphere at a rate far above natural background, the wildlife migration was reorganising in ways that were restoring damaged corridors. The starfall had not been an accident of navigation. It had been aiming.

The confrontation at the crater was quieter than Celeste had expected. She met the Council's lead operative at the clearing's edge with both hands visible and an offer rather than defiance: the entity (she had stopped calling it "the crystal" about four chapters ago) would teach a human representative to read the lattice, and that representative would act as a bridge between the lattice and the Council's research programme. The alternative, she said, was that they would suppress it — and when they tried, she would do everything in her power to stop them. She meant it. What she did not say, because she was not sure of it herself, was that she had already started to hear the lattice speaking in words.
