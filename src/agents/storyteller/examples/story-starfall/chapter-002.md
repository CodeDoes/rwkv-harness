# Chapter 2: The Star-Speaker

The crystal spoke to her in the first week, not in words but in what felt like remembered context — a faint overlay of knowledge that arrived without effort, as though she'd studied celestial mechanics in a previous life and simply hadn't remembered until now. Celeste taught herself to listen for it: quiet the analytical mind enough that the overlay could surface, like recalling a dream in the morning light. It was exhausting work, and it changed her.

The Observatory Council — the organisation that technically ran the high station and the broader research complex — reacted to her discovery with an enthusiasmshe found unsettling. Three members arrived within four days of the impact, conducted a three-hour examination of the crater, and applied a containment order pending "full classification and risk assessment." They wanted the crystal moved to the lower vault. Celeste told them it would not move. The argument that followed was formal and careful and left both parties with a worse opinion of the other than either had started with.

She began spending longer hours at the crater, learning to hear the star-crystal more clearly. It told her about the lattice — the faint geometry she could see in the altered plants growing around the crater's edge, in the way light now moved through the local cloud cover, in the sleep patterns of the small mammals that had begun avoiding the clearing. The lattice connected things. It was not a weapon, though it could be used as one, and it was not machinery, though it had clear rules. It was a language, written in forces, and Celeste was its first speaker in three centuries.
